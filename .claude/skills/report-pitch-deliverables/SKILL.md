# Report, pitch, and SDK statement

Explanation is worth 20% of the rubric — as much as comparison-and-scaling, more than the classical baseline. It is scored on whether a judge can follow the reasoning and whether every claim survives scrutiny. The house style of the whole entry: **rigor and honesty over ambition** — the brief says a modest, well-delimited, fully reproducible result outscores an impressive unverifiable claim. Write everything in that voice.

## Technical report (PDF, ≤8 pages, mandatory)

Suggested structure with page budget — the limits are tight, so allocate by rubric weight:

1. **Problem & real data** (~1 pg): fault-zone partitioning as Max-Cut; ICE-derived instance with named substations; simplifications table (from `grid-maxcut-modeling`).
2. **Formulation** (~1 pg): QUBO with sign convention stated, brute-force verification mentioned explicitly.
3. **Methods** (~1.5 pg): classical baselines (brute force, greedy, GW), QAOA setup, warm-start experimental design, platform (Selene / H2 emulator — say *emulator*, never *hardware*).
4. **Results** (~2 pg): r-vs-p with error bars and GW/greedy reference lines; warm-start Δ(p) win or lose; scaling across ≥2 sizes with extrapolation labeled as extrapolation.
5. **Limitations** (~0.5–1 pg, **mandatory**): see below.
6. **SDG impact** (~0.5 pg): causal chains to SDG 7/9/13 tied to the specific partition found (not name-drops).
7. **Reproducibility** (~0.5 pg): repo URL, single command, seed policy, cached quantum runs with job IDs.

Every number in the report must be regenerated by the repo's entry point (`reproducibility-pipeline`) and every claim traceable to a measurement or citation (`quantum-code-audit` before submission).

### Limitations section — pre-written skeleton (fill numbers on day 3)

The brief makes this section obligatory and its absence a red flag. Four statements, drafted on day 1:

1. QAOA does not currently beat Goemans-Williamson for Max-Cut on any instance; at p=1 its guarantee (0.6924, **3-regular graphs only** — do not claim it for the grid instance) is strictly below GW's 0.878. *Our measured gap: ___ ± ___.*
2. The instances are aggregated simplifications of the real ICE network; large-instance edges use a documented connectivity proxy.
3. Emulator results are not noisy-hardware results.
4. The warm-start effect is reported win or lose; *observed Δ(p): ___*.

## Red-flag self-audit (run against the final draft)

The brief names these as scoring red flags — check each explicitly:
- [ ] No "quantum advantage" claim anywhere (including implied by chart framing or pitch wording).
- [ ] Limitations section present and consistent with every other section (a warm-start paragraph that hints at beating GW contradicts it — see `warm-start-qaoa`).
- [ ] No cherry-picking: all statistics are mean ± std over ≥5 seeds; no best-of-N presented as typical.
- [ ] Raw cost never reported without the approximation ratio.
- [ ] Emulator labeled as emulator.

## Pitch (5 minutes)

Judges reward a defensible story, not spectacle. A timing that matches the rubric's weights:

- **0:00–0:45** Problem: cascading blackouts, islanding, why partitioning is NP-hard — with the real Costa Rica map.
- **0:45–2:00** What we built: canonical pipeline, real ICE data, three classical baselines, QAOA on the official emulator. (This covers 65% of the rubric — give it proportional airtime.)
- **2:00–3:30** Results: the r-vs-p plot with error bars is the hero visual; state the QAOA-GW gap out loud — volunteering honesty preempts the obvious judge question. Warm-start as the controlled-experiment story.
- **3:30–4:15** Interactive map: remove a line, watch re-islanding, per-island balance/renewables (the SDG close).
- **4:15–5:00** Limitations + reproducibility: "one command regenerates everything" is a strong closing line.

Demo insurance: pre-record or screenshot the map interaction; never depend on live Wi-Fi.

### Q&A preparation — questions to rehearse
- "Why doesn't QAOA beat GW?" → the limitations answer, verbatim, with your measured gap.
- "Are your edges just geographic proximity?" → the adjacency-vs-line-topology distinction (a specialist trap the model already defends against).
- "Would this scale to the full national grid?" → measured scaling on 2–3 sizes + H3 hierarchical aggregation as the *labeled* extrapolation path; 26-qubit emulator ceiling acknowledged.
- "Why these edge weights / this ε / this optimizer?" → every parameter has a documented reason or a stated default.

## SDK statement (≤200 words, mandatory)

Written from notes taken *during* development, not reconstructed at the end (see `guppy-h2` for what to log). Structure: what was used (Guppy/Qiskit split and why) → what worked → what didn't → what was missing. Honest friction reports score; a fabricated smooth experience reads as not having tried. If Guppy was attempted and abandoned, that attempt is the statement's most valuable content.

Route the full deliverable set through `quantum-code-audit` before submission — it exists to catch exactly the claims a judge would.
